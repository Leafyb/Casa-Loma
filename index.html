Welcome to Casa Loma: The Castle on the hill

Casa Loma (Spanish for "Hill House") is a Gothic Revival style mansion and garden in midtown Toronto, Ontario, Canada, that is now a historic house museum and landmark. At 98 rooms covering 64,700 square feet (6,011 m2), it was the largest private residence in Canada. It was constructed from 1911 to 1914 as a residence for financier Sir Henry Pellatt. The architect was E. J. Lennox, who designed several other city landmarks. Casa Loma sits at an elevation of 140 metres (460 ft) above sea level. The house cost about $3.5 million and took 300 workers three years to build. Notable amenities included an elevator, an oven large enough to cook an ox, two vertical passages for pipe organs, a central vacuum, two secret passages in Pellatt's ground-floor office. A pool and three bowling alleys in the basement were never completed. - Wikipedia

Sir Henry Pellatt filled Casa Loma with priceless artwork from Canada and around the world. Casa Loma stood as a monument to its creator – it surpassed any private home in North America. With soaring battlements and secret passageways, it paid homage to the castles and knights of days gone by. To this day it remains one of the only true castles in North America. Sir Henry Pellatt’s numerous business and military connections demanded entertaining on a large scale. Casa Loma’s romantic borrowing from the past, tempered by necessary modern day conveniences, provided the perfect setting. - The Casa Loma Website

About Sir Henry Pellatt

Major-General Sir Henry Mill Pellatt, KCVO (January 6, 1859 – March 8, 1939) was a Canadian financier and soldier.

He is notable for his role in bringing hydro-electricity to Toronto, Ontario, for the first time, and also for his large château in Toronto, called Casa Loma, which was the biggest private residence ever constructed in Canada.

Pellatt was born in Kingston, Canada West (now Ontario), the son of Henry Pellatt (1830–1909), a Glasgow-born stockbroker in Toronto, and Emma Mary Pellatt (born in Holland). His great-grandfather was the famous glassmaker Apsley Pellatt.



Pellatt had three sisters and two brothers, Fred Pellatt and Mill Pellatt. His sisters were Mary Kate, Marian Maria and Emily Mountford Pellatt.

He was educated at Upper Canada College before leaving in 1876 to join his father's stock brokerage company, Pellatt and Osler, as a clerk. In 1882, Pellatt's father and Osler parted ways, and Pellatt completed his apprenticeship and became a full member of the stock exchange.

Pellatt married twice, first to Mary Dodgson, his high school sweetheart, in Toronto in 1882 and, after Mary's death in 1924, to Catharine Welland Merritt in Toronto in 1927, which lasted until her death in 1929. With his first wife, he had one son, Reginald, who was born in 1885.

Pellatt enlisted as a rifleman with The Queen's Own Rifles of Canada on November 2, 1876. He rose through the ranks and eventually became the Commanding Officer. In 1905, he was created a Knight Bachelor by King Edward VII for his service with The Queen's Own Rifles of Canada.

In 1910, Pellatt took the entire 600-man regiment (including its horses) to England for military training at his expense, to mark the Regiment's 50th anniversary. The military exercises lasted from August 13 to October 3, 1910.

Pellatt later served as the regiment's Honorary Colonel and was promoted to the rank of Major-General upon his retirement from the regiment. In addition, he was made a Commander of the Royal Victorian Order (CVO) in 1910.

From 1911 to 1923, he was the Knight Principal of the Imperial Society of Knights Bachelor. - Wikipedia (with some changes made in an attempt to make it shorter)

History

In 1903, Henry Pellatt purchased 25 lots from developers Kertland and Rolf. Pellatt commissioned architect E. J. Lennox to design Casa Loma. In 1911, construction beganstarting with the massive stables, potting shed and Hunting Lodge (a.k.a. coach-house) a few hundred feet north of the main building. The Hunting Lodge is a two-storey 4,380-square-foot (407 m2) house with servants' quarters. As soon as the stable complex was completed, Pellatt sold his summer house in Scarborough to his son and moved to the Hunting Lodge. But then due to the start of World War I, construction was halted.



Most of the third floor was left unfinished, and today serves as the Regimental Museum for The Queen's Own Rifles of Canada. Which Pellatt joined as a Rifleman and rose through the ranks to become the Commanding Officer.

During the depression that followed the war, the City of Toronto increased Casa Loma's property taxes from $600 per year to $1,000 a month, and Pellatt, already experiencing financial difficulties, auctioned off $1.5 million in art and $250,000 in furnishings. Pellatt was able to enjoy life in the castle for less than ten years, leaving in 1923.

In the late 1920s, investors operated Casa Loma for a short time as a luxury hotel. During Prohibition, it became a popular nightspot for wealthy Americans. The city seized Casa Loma in 1924 for unpaid taxes, and for years, the building was left vacant.

In the 1930s, CFRB broadcaster Claire Wallace spent a night at Casa Loma to gather material for a story about ghosts and supernatural phenomena, and she later broadcast an appeal to save the old building from demolition. Her broadcast was heard by the vice-president of a local Kiwanis Club, and subsequently Casa Loma was leased in 1937 by the Kiwanis Club of West Toronto, later Kiwanis Club of Casa Loma (KCCL) , which began operating the castle under a sole-source contract as a tourist destination. KCCL managed Casa Loma for 74 years, until 2011.

During World War II, the stables were used to conceal research and production of sonar, and for construction of sonar devices. The area was closed, behind an "Under Repairs" sign. The suggestion that the stables were under renovation allowed workers of the secret facility to come and go without suspicion.

From 1997 until 2012, the castle underwent a 15-year, $33-million exterior restoration largely funded by the city. In 2011, the city temporarily resumed management of Casa Loma, because of KCCls inability to help pay for upgrades, and began welcoming bids from the private sector in its search for a new operator.

In January 2014, the city entered a new long-term lease and operating agreement with Liberty Entertainment Group, led by President and CEO Nick Di Donato, which agreed to spend $7.4 million to continue the castle's upgrades. - Wikipedia (with some changes made in an attempt to make it shorter)

The Castle Layout

Sir Henry imported artisans from Europe to design much of the furniture and other features of the castle. Casa Loma has five acres of gardens. An underground tunnel connects Casa Loma to the Hunting Lodge and to the stables (garage, potting shed, stalls, carriage room and tack rooms). - Wikipedia (with some changes made in an attempt to make it shorter)

A visit to the Castle

A visit to Casa Loma is a thrilling experience that takes you back in time, which shows us the rich and sometimes not so rich history of Canada. The history of Casa Loma is filled with the love of a man of ambition. The life, success and tragedies of Sir Henry Pellatt are embedded into the core of this beautiful building. Sir Henry Pellatt may have died almost penniless but the success of his life and gifts he bestowed upon Canada are countless. Casa Loma stands as a proud memorial to this great man.

Upon entering the castle, there is a beautiful garden, which is a wonderful place to take pictures with your family and friends. After taking as many amazing pictures as the heart desires, we move on to the entrance gate of the Castle. There we buy our tickets and enter the great hall of the castle. On our right is the oak room, which can only be viewed not entered. Next to us our the stairs that lead upstairs to the second floor. And on the left is a hallway leading to the conservatory. Next to that is the door to the library. And in front is the doors that lead outside. On a table on the side they are giving free audio tour devices which you can borrow and then return later.

So we have borrowed an audio tour device, which you hold to your ear to listen to some facts and history about each place you visit. We enter the library, which is on the left, and we see an area to take pictures and a large bookshelf on the right. There at the far end is the dining room and next to that is the conservatory. There is another door leading outside. The floor is lined with an intricate pattern of wood and the ceiling has the coat of arms of Sir Henry Pellatt and his wife's.

We then take a walk outside, look up and admire the towers of the castle. We walk down the stairs to the garden below. We explore a bit and find a passage through the woods which takes us around in loop. We come back inside. We take a peek at the dining room and then enter the conservatory.

Entering the conservatory, we see plants on the side walls, and a fountain at the far end. We notice a large dome in the middle of the room, with beautiful and intricate patterns. We find out that the dome is actually is actually lit by electric lights and not sun light because then Sir Henry Pellatt could enjoy the color of the dome day and night.

We exit the conservatory through the other door, and find ourselves in the corridor we saw when we first entered the castle. We move along the corridor, looking at rooms on our right side, we find the entrance to Sir Henry Pellat’s study. The room has a beautiful carpet, chairs and a bookshelf on left. There is a fireplace on the right and we see two hidden doors, now open on either side of the fireplace. One leading to the basement, and the other to the second floor. We go upstairs.

On the second floor there is another large corridor. We see the entrances to Sir Henry Pellatt’s and Lady Pellatt’s rooms. They each have their own. Lady Pellatt’s room is larger than Sir Henry Pellatt's. The bathrooms are filled with the latest technologies of the time. Across the corridor we enter the guest rooms. After exiting the guest rooms we walk down the corridor and take a peek at the elevator which is only the employees are allowed to use. Then we see a balcony overlooking the great hall. We walk a little bit more and see stairs to the third floor, but there are more rooms to see so we continue. To the right is room and to the left is the Windsor room. We return and then go up the stairs to the third floor. We see that the corridor is being used as a museum for the Queen’s Own Rifles. We walk along all the way to the right and left and see that all the rooms exhibit the Queen's Own Rifles.

Along the corridor we see a staircase that leads to the towers of the castle. We see that there is a small pathway that leads to two of the castle towers. On the east side, there is the Scotish tower and on the west side the Norman tower. The Scotish tower has an inclosed top. The Norman tower, on the other hand has no roof, and provides breath-taking views of the city of Toronto and is a great place to take photos.

After visiting the towers, we go back down to the first floor. On the first floor next to the entrance is the staircase to the basement. In the basement we see the gift shop right in front. Next to that we see the wine cellar . And then on the other side of the corridor we see the unfinished pool, which has been converted into a movie theater. On the movie theater we watch a biography of Sir Henry Pellatt.

After exiting the pool/ movie theater, we walk to the other side of the corridor we see that there is a tunnel, next to the wine cellar, the tunnel exhibits a picture museum called the The Dark Side Tunnel Exhibits. At the end of the tunnel we see a staircase that leads upwards into a room that has a bouncy castle. Next to that, we see another room that has been converted into a vintage car museum. After we look at the cars, we turn around and go to the stables, there aren’t any horses there though. After the stables, we go back down into the tunnel. While we walk down the tunnel we see another door, which takes us up to a greenhouse/potting shed. The potting shed has various plants for us to examine and look at. After spending some time there we go back down the tunnel and return to the basement. We go back up the staircase and turn around and leave the castle.
